    Myanmar Airpolution

    Along with its rapid development since 2011, Myanmar has been experiencing a rise in air pollution in urban areas. Yangon and Mandalay are experiencing increased urbanization from rural-urban migration and growing density as the urban population growth has been faster than spatial growth. In 2017, air pollution is estimated to attribute to more than 45,000 deaths in Myanmar. As a risk factor for death, air pollution is higher in Myanmar than in other countries in the region, almost twice the average for Southeast Asia. Yet actual data with high reliability on air quality in Myanmar is lacking, and arrangements for monitoring of air quality and pollution control are still in their infancy.

    Wildfires and Smoke

    Wildfires are often described as uncontrollable fires that spread through woodlands or vegetation. Itâ€™s both environmentally destructive and health deteriorating. Unfortunately, Myanmar had the highest burn rate in all of South-East Asia, and ranked 11th globally. There are many reasons that cause a wildfire to start. Naturally, it can start from a bolt lightning, a dry climate, or a volcanic eruption. But most wildfires start from man-made causes such as slash-and-burn farming methods, cooking in woods and burning trash. Wildfires can heavily impact air quality. The green vegetation in those areas is often credited for air purification, yet when cast on fire, they ironically release burdens of ash and smoke that contain micro dust-particles and toxic gases to become an even bigger agent of air pollution. The release pollutants irritate our eyes and respiratory tract, and can worsen our underlying heart and lung condition, if we have any. The most vulnerable to wildfire smoke are the elderly, the pregnant, and children.

    Myanmar coal mining 'causing environmental disasters'

    Farmers in Myanmar's Shan State say coal mining operations are causing widespread environmental damage. Bordering China and Laos, Shan State is home to several ethnic groups and is only partly under the control of Myanmar's government. Activists warn that pollution levels are increasing as mining operations across the state continue to grow.
